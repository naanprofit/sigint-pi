📐 Advanced SDR - Antenna Array & Direction Finding

Configure multiple SDR receivers as a direction-finding array. Position antennas around a protected area to triangulate drone RF sources.

⚡ Quick Setup Presets
4-Sector: 4 antennas at N/S/E/W, one device per sector
4-Sector Dual: 8 antennas, 2 per sector (RTL-SDR for EMI + HackRF for controller packets)
KrakenSDR Center: 5-element coherent DF array at center
Full 8-Device + Kraken: Complete recommended setup -- 4 sector pairs + central KrakenSDR DF node
📋 Recommended Setup Guide
Layer 1: Fixed Sector Sentries (4x RTL-SDR + 4x HackRF)
Per direction (N/S/E/W), mount two antennas at same azimuth, 1-3 ft vertical separation:
  • RTL-SDR + wideband LPDA -- continuous EMI baseline, anomaly detection, motor harmonics. LPDA = wideband AND directional (80 MHz - 2 GHz typical). RTL-SDR V4: ~$40, covers 24-1766 MHz.
  • HackRF + band-targeted directional -- recommended split: 2x sub-GHz yagi (900/915 MHz, ~$50-$120 ea) + 2x dual-band panel (2.4/5 GHz, ~$25-$60 ea). HackRF: 1 MHz - 6 GHz, half-duplex, 20 MSPS.
Use short feedlines with ferrite chokes. Avoid splitting one antenna into both radios -- two antennas per sector is cleaner (passive splitter costs signal).
Layer 2: Central KrakenSDR DF Node
KrakenSDR ($749) + Krakentenna matched set ($249) = $998
5-channel coherent RTL-SDR on single oscillator, 24-1766 MHz. Provides real phase-based bearing estimation.
Key difference: Sectors say "east is hottest." Kraken says "bearing 073 degrees."
Two DF methods:
Sector method (your N/S/E/W) = "which antenna sees it strongest?" -- coarse bearing.
Coherent DF (KrakenSDR) = "what is the phase difference across synchronized antennas?" -- precise bearing.
Altitude Coverage: Ground to 800 ft
At 0.25 mi, 800 ft up = ~30 deg elevation. At 0.5 mi = ~13.5 deg.
Antenna beamwidth matters: A 2.4 GHz panel may have 90 deg azimuth but only 22 deg elevation. Some 5 GHz sectors are 11-12 deg vertical. Yagis/LPDAs are wider: 40-100 deg vertical.
Use: RTL side = wideband LPDA (wide vertical). HackRF side = moderate-gain panel or yagi (not ultra-thin elevation). Tilt slightly upward if nearby overhead matters more than far horizon.
Fiber-Optic Drone Detection
Fiber-optic drones have no RF control link to intercept. Detection relies on incidental EMI from motors, ESCs, switching regulators.
Weak EMI only (motors/ESCs): tens to a few hundred meters in good conditions.
Stronger RF (telemetry/video): much farther, and where KrakenSDR DF excels.
Best approach: Fused alert pipeline: sector energy anomaly appears > repeats over multiple sweeps > matching activity in same sector > optional acoustic/thermal cue agrees > raise confidence and update track.
EMI alone is not a reliable primary method. Treat it as confidence input, not proof.
Baseline Subtraction & EMI Detection Range
Recording a clean RF baseline per sector/band/time-of-day, then comparing live sweeps against it, improves contrast for weak anomalies. It does not cancel thermal noise or remove time-varying interference.
What it buys: new narrowband spikes, repeated harmonics, pulsed switching noise, directional transients become visible even near the noise floor. Fewer false alarms, cleaner "this is new" detections, better operator updates.
Estimated detection range with baseline subtraction:
Incidental EMI (motors/ESCs) Without baseline: ~50-150m With baseline: ~100-250m (good), ~250-400m (favorable)
Real RF emitters (telemetry/video) Hundreds of meters Up to kilometers (where KrakenSDR DF excels)
Ranges are planning estimates. Highly dependent on target signature repeatability, site background stability, and shielding.
Works best when: target EMI is repetitive, spectrally distinctive, directionally localized to one sector.
Works poorly when: target emission is broadband and very weak, environment is busy and constantly changing.
More HackRFs = faster? No. More HackRFs = more parallel observation (more bands or sectors at once). Each HackRF is still one half-duplex 20 MSPS radio. 4 HackRFs = 4 slices simultaneously, not 4x faster per sweep.
